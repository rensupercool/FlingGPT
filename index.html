Foundations

Core Mechanics

FTAP's combat wasn't designed — it was discovered. None of these mechanics were put in by the developer; players built the entire system themselves.

Flinging

Not a built-in mechanic — naturally discovered. Grab your opponent, move them to hit the floor to ragdoll them, then look behind you and fling them low (not touching the ground) so they can't clutch. Done with a fast flick.

Ragdolling

An often-overlooked mechanic. Happens when you grab someone at decently high speed — basically any air-to-air fight. Makes flings more consistent and much harder to clutch out of.

Flung Physics

Flung players can barely move laterally unless not ragdolled. Most hold S and face the direction they were thrown to lessen the distance. Under 2k ping it's barely affected by latency.

Aiming

Works like an FPS but players move extremely fast — timing is just as important as raw aim, if not more so. Proximity-based with a 30-stud grab range. You can't grab from across the map.

Game Format

There are no built-in PvP game modes. All competitive play is done by joining a public lobby and finding people, or arranging private 1v1s, 2v2s, 3v3s, and FFAs on Discord. 1v1s are most common; FFAs are popular too. The map rarely updates — seasonal maps like Christmas, Valentine's Day, and Independence Day were made years in advance.

Mobility

Movement

Every single movement technique in FTAP was player-invented. No dashing, no bunny hopping, no built-in PvP mechanics — just physics, creativity, and the community.

Physics System

The movement system acts like a Garry's Mod physics gun attached to your pallet, with a 30-stud range (same as your grab range). Grab the pallet further back for more speed with less control. Grab it high and scroll less for precision.

Speed & Control

Scroll out to go faster. Grab the pallet further back for raw speed at the cost of control. Grab it really high and scroll back to drag yourself upward and backward. All movement is physics-driven with no shortcuts.

The Dive

Look down and scroll out to drop fast. A core move used in nearly every playstyle. High-fps players can dive and stop almost instantly due to the physics advantage — making them incredibly difficult to fight.

No Built-In Mechanics

FTAP has zero built-in movement tech — no dashing, jumping, wall-running, or PvP abilities. Every technique was invented through pure experimentation with the physics engine by the player community.

System

AG · Anti-Grab

A game-level distance check designed to stop exploits. Works for its intended purpose, but frequently punishes legitimate competitive play.

What Is AG?

AG (anti-grab) is a distance check built by the game's developer horomori to stop infinite-reach exploits. It does what it's designed to do — but it also cancels legitimate grabs when someone moves too fast at high ping. Your grab gets outright wiped, which is painful after fighting hard to get the position for it.

When It Triggers

AG fires when someone moves too fast at high ping, dashes back hard, or dives right as you rush them. The physics can push a player outside the valid grab range before the grab registers, canceling it outright.

Rupasu's Mastery

Rupasu built his entire elite slow playstyle around baiting AGs. He positions so that rushing him triggers AG on the attacker — then he stops and grabs while they're in the AG animation. This is a big part of what makes him one of the hardest players to touch.

Survival

Clutching

FTAP's most ping-sensitive mechanic. Sub-100ms players have a significant edge that makes them extremely difficult to fling and keep down.

Double Pallet Clutch

Sub-100 ping players can look down and spawn a pallet under themselves while being flung, jump on it for a height boost, then spawn a second pallet and grab it normally. Normal-ping players simply can't execute this. The only real counters are a very low fling (doesn't work sub-40 ping) or dunking.

Dunking

Throwing the opponent downward — into water. Eliminates most clutch options. Hard to execute consistently since most fight spots don't have water on all sides, just one.

Tabbing (Exploit)

Holding right-click on the Roblox window while being flung causes the game to freeze and bug, sending you to the nearest solid floor. Widely frowned upon. Considered an exploit, not a legitimate clutch technique.

Shuriken Clutch

The shuriken is a sticky item in FTAP. Spawn it under yourself and it sticks to you — when flung, it's right below you, so look down and grab it. Simulates a double pallet clutch but even more reliable. Extremely difficult to counter.

Combat Identity

Playstyles

Everything branches from fast or slow. Hybrid players exist but are rare. The starting foundation for everyone is center-mass grabbing and strafing left and right.

Fast Archetypes

High FPS

In FTAP, FPS literally equals speed. Players at 1k+ fps get a massive speed and control boost over the typical sub-200 crowd. They spam the dive and can stop almost instantly. Fighting them is genuinely rough. The fps advantage is unknown to most sub-expert players — effectively pay-to-win with zero downsides.

Aggressive-Fast

Decent aim, relentless rush spam. These players rarely do anything fancy — just go and go. High pressure, low variety. Predictable at higher skill levels but suffocating for anyone below expert who doesn't know how to handle it.

Controlled-Fast

LostNPC's archetype — mid pallet grab, high scrolling. Insanely consistent. Any technique can be layered into this style without breaking the flow. Players who use this almost never drop below expert 2, with rare exceptions going higher.

Slow Archetypes

Very Slow-Slow

Chamo's signature. Almost no movement except the dive. Rush them and they dash back and grab you — turning your own aggression against you. Deceptively effective at high skill where the baits are perfectly timed.

Hybrid-Slow

Not actually slow players — they convert to slow play whenever you rush or overcommit. Slower movement means more reaction time, so they weaponize deceleration as a defensive tool, switching modes on demand.

Elite Slow (Rupasu)

The most refined and dangerous slow style. Rush rupasu and he dives under you and grabs you on the way down — nearly unheard of. The only theoretical counter is baiting his dive, then diving yourself to break his timing. Against him specifically, even that barely works.

How to Beat a Slow Player

You can't just rush them straight up — that's exactly what they want. Bait out their dive: rush at them, make them commit to the dodge, then YOU stop rushing and do your own dive to break their timing. Grab them while they're resetting. Against rupasu specifically, this barely works either.

Pallet Throwing

A high-risk technique. Rainz popularized it — grab the pallet, throw it at the opponent, grab it again, throw it again, repeat until you've completely gotten behind them. Hard to grab while doing this, which is why rainz stops moving when he goes for the actual grab. Semi-unheard of as a consistent technique, but devastating when it works.

Timeline

Ranking History

The evolution of FTAP's ranking system — from a zerogath-run gold standard to the fragmented independent era of today.

Era 1

Fling Enthusiasts (FE)

The original community ranking server, founded and run by zerogath. He evaluated every player personally — ranks from this era were considered 100% accurate. The undisputed gold standard of FTAP ranking. Died around 2024.

Era 2

Fling Syndicate (FS)

Replaced Fling Enthusiasts. Zerogath-controlled initially, then handed off to spence. Rankings shifted from zerogath personally to high-skill players — still around 90% accurate, but not the same guarantee. Eventually abandoned by spence and went dark.

Since ~2024

Independent Era

No official ranking server. Known players and high-ranked individuals (expert 2+) started ranking independently. Quality is wildly inconsistent. Has devolved into nearly anyone ranking — including apprentices. Whole clans are known for systematic overranking: TCU, WDG, and various Russian clans.

Present

Current Trust Standards

Rankings are generally only trusted from known community members or players at expert 2 and above. Clan-affiliated rankings and unknown sources are treated with heavy skepticism. No centralized authority exists.
