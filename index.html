Fundamentals

The core of FTAP

The supplied notes describe FTAP as a player-created movement and combat game where speed, timing, aim, and pallet control matter more than any built-in combat system.

Core mechanic

Flinging

Flinging is described as a naturally discovered mechanic. You grab the opponent, move them into the floor to ragdoll them, then flick them low enough that they cannot recover cleanly.

Mechanical pressure

Aiming and timing

The notes frame aiming like an FPS problem, except both players are moving rapidly. Because reach is proximity-based, timing is just as important as raw crosshair placement.

Movement identity

Pallet control

Movement is built around grabbing the pallet at different heights and scroll distances. More scroll gives speed and less control, while higher grabs and dragging back give more control and lift.

Anti-grab matters

AG changes fight decisions

AG is a distance check used to stop infinite reach. In the notes it is one of the biggest sources of frustration for PvP players, especially when people move too fast or when dives and dashes back create canceled grabs at the worst possible moment.

Clutching shapes kills

Why low flings and dunks matter

Clutching is presented as one of FTAP's most unfair mechanics because ping heavily affects it. That is why very low flings and dunking are important: they reduce or remove the chance of a recovery.

Playstyles

The fast and slow split

According to the notes, almost every style branches from fast or slow play, with only occasional hybrid players sitting between those two poles.

Fast archetypes

  • High FPS: extreme speed and control, dive-heavy, widely seen as unfair.
  • Aggressive-fast: constant rush pressure with straightforward offense.
  • Controlled-fast: cleaner flow, mid pallet grabbing, adaptable to many moves.

Slow archetypes

  • Very slow: minimal movement, waits for overcommitment, punishes rushes.
  • Hybrid-slow: converts to slower play when the opponent makes a bad push.
  • Elite slow: dive-under grabs, AG baiting, and forcing the attacker into bad timing.

How to pressure a slow player

The notes say you do not beat strong slow players by rushing straight at them. You bait the dive, force the defensive movement, then change your own timing with a stop or dive so you grab during their reset.

Rank ladder

How the notes describe FTAP progression

The hierarchy goes from casual all the way to fling god, with the middle and upper tiers becoming more specialized in aim, movement, consistency, and real match impact.

Casual Entry level

Can fling, barely flies, usually stays near the ground, and generally lacks consistent aim or control.

Apprentice Low / Mid / High

Can fly, can grab straightforward opportunities, and may show the first signs of skillful movement.

Skillful Tiers I - III

Where players start getting good: stable flying, easy grabs, basic jukes, and much more deliberate movement.

Expert Tiers I - III

Usually server standouts with high-level movement or high-level aim, and often enough consistency to swing fights.

Advanced Tiers I - III

Very rare. Strong at both aim and movement in most cases, with only a few exceptions or suspected over-ranks.

Fling God Levels, not tiers

The rarest status in the notes, reserved for the most notable names in the game's history.

Advanced tech

Pallet tech and fight manipulation

The second note set focuses on techniques tied to double clicking and the "cool method." These cards keep the ideas readable while preserving the playstyle fit and rough practicality described in the source.

Fast playstyles

Stutter-step

A speed reset that heavily breaks rhythm by abruptly stopping motion in place. The notes frame it as a strong rhythm breaker that pairs well with diving and other fast pressure.

Fast playstyles

Pallet resetting

Lets the player switch direction extremely quickly while slightly changing elevation. It is presented as a strong control tool for unpredictable fast movement.

Instant grabbing

Crosshair-over-opponent double-click grabs that are described as visually clean and especially deadly when paired with movement that does the aiming for you.

AG bypassing

Triple grab attempts designed to beat distance-check failures by giving the grab multiple chances to connect. The notes present it as experimental and not yet broadly practical.

Possibility 20% with CM / 0% without
Practicality 40% with CM / 0% without
Unpredictable styles

Pallet tapping

Early release pressure followed by a delayed or short pallet tap to fake timing. The technique is useful because the exact pause length can vary so much that opponents misread the rush.

Possibility 80% with CM / 60% without
Practicality 60% with CM / 20% without
Slow playstyles

Pallet flicking

Flick-based grabs done while never fully letting go of the pallet. The notes describe it as difficult, stylish, and more realistic for slower speeds or smaller flick angles.

Possibility 70% on small flicks / 20% on large
Practicality 60% with CM / 0% without
Fast playstyles

Insta diving

A dive chained into pallet reset to refresh momentum and let the player rush again immediately. The notes present it as a likely core tool for faster high-FPS movement.

Possibility 100% with CM / 20% without
Practicality 80% with CM / 10% without
Unconfirmed

Scroll faking

Described as a possible point-of-view desync where the opponent sees a max-scroll state without matching physical movement. The source explicitly marks this as unconfirmed and very inconsistent.

Possibility ~10% with CM scroll
Practicality High only if it becomes consistent

Other named tech in the notes

  • Throw resetting: a weaker reset using the throw mechanic.
  • Stunning: forces the opponent to re-grab while you pressure them.
  • Player clutching: body-based clutching while both players are grabbed.
  • Pre-strafing: leaning into the direction you want before the actual turn.

What "CM" means here

The uploaded notes define CM as short for "cool method." Several advanced techniques are described as far more practical with CM than without it, especially speed resets and instant grab variants.

Big picture takeaway

Most of the listed tech is not about raw speed alone. It is about rhythm breaking, direction switching, AG manipulation, and forcing the opponent to read the wrong timing.

Clans and format

How the scene is organized

The notes describe FTAP PvP as community organized: public fights, private 1v1s, occasional team modes, and clan ecosystems built outside the game itself.

How people actually play PvP

  • There are no official PvP modes in the source notes.
  • Most fights happen in public servers or arranged private sets.
  • 1v1s are most common, while 2v2s, 3v3s, and FFAs still happen.
  • The map changes very little, outside occasional event maps.

Clan snapshot

  • IVC: former expert-heavy roster with many big names.
  • GEM: elite player group gathered around major historical names.
  • TCU: described negatively, with accusations of cheating and bad ranking.
  • KL / JBL / UE / WDG / AS: active or legacy community clusters with different standards and reputations.
